## Plot in one breath

A nervous client wants Lovejoy to authenticate a piece of Japanese lacquerware, which immediately gets stolen, which immediately involves a dead body, which immediately involves Lovejoy drinking Champagne in an aristos attic and then culminates in a whisk soaked boat chase..for some reason.

## Opening Scene Observations

From the moment the opening credits start — the music, the bucolic pictures, the coolness of Ian McShane — I was hooked.

We open on metal filings falling from a small brass object. Then the camera pulls back and we get the reveal: Lovejoy himself, followed by the title card, *based on the books by Jonathan Gash* — and broken glass.

It's understated, but majestic. Like a duck landing.

Straight into Lovejoy at the wheel, old Volvo smoking away, completely at ease. Huey Lewis and the News playing. Around him: barns, fields, a beautiful thatched cottage that looks like it belongs on a postcard. Which, as we later find out, it sort of does — his "borrowed" living situation a nod to disenfranchised 2020s Britain. Ahead of his time.

We're then straight into Eric — headphones on, blasting something hair-metal 80s (what is that, Pantera? Megadeth?), completely disconnected from everything around him. His butcher dad despairing of his vegetarian son, and a quick Thatcher jab just to make sure we know it's the mid 80s.

And then, just over four minutes in, we get the first proper Lovejoy moment.

He turns to camera:

> "If home is where the heart is… then this is home for me."

Standing in an antiques fair.

That's it — four and a half minutes in:

- fourth wall broken
- character established
- entire premise laid out

No build-up, no explanation. Just confidence.

This divvy is in.

## Review

It is genuinely strange how confidently *Lovejoy* arrives. There is no fumbling around for tone in this opening episode — within about ninety seconds you have a wink to camera, an antique nobody seems to want to explain properly, a vaguely menacing client, and a Morris Minor. Whoever made this knew exactly what show they were making.

What's more surprising on a rewatch is how quickly the central trick lands. McShane is given approximately three lines of expository dialogue and then is allowed, simply, to look at things. The camera lingers, he tilts his head, and the entire premise of the series is conveyed without anyone having to say the word "divvy". This is unbelievably efficient television.

The plot itself is a very polite murder. Someone has died, but it has happened tidily, off-screen, in the manner of a dinner party guest who has had to leave early. The actual mystery is small and slightly underwritten — the lacquerware is mostly an excuse to get Lovejoy into a country house, and then out of it again, slightly faster than he went in. None of this matters. The show is not about the case, it's about the loitering.

The supporting cast turn up like a checklist. Eric is introduced and is immediately doing too much. Tinker is introduced and is immediately in a pub. Lady Jane is introduced and the show very politely refuses to clarify what is going on between her and our hero, an ambiguity it will protect with the ferocity of a Victorian aunt for the next eight years.

The look of the thing is extraordinary. East Anglia, in summer, in 1986, on what appears to have been a generous BBC film budget — every shot of a hedge looks like a postcard your nan would refuse to throw away. It will not always look this expensive, but here it does, and it sets a baseline that the show keeps gesturing back to.

It is not a perfect episode. The pacing sags slightly in the middle act, when the script remembers it is supposed to be a mystery and dutifully shuffles some clues around. There's a scene in a barn that feels like it was written on the morning of the shoot. But the closing fifteen minutes — in which Lovejoy gets the antique, doesn't get the money, and gets to do a small piece to camera about how that is, on balance, fine — are about as confident an ending as a pilot can manage.

## Lovejoy Units

Five direct addresses to camera, by my count. One of them is genuinely affecting, three of them are a smirk, and one is delivered while leaning on the bonnet of the Morris in a manner that, if you are of a certain disposition, may cause you to remember a thing about yourself you had not previously consciously known.

## Divvy Moment

A strange woman knocks on the door. The very first thing Lovejoy says to her is to invite her in — before she's introduced herself, before he knows what she wants, before any sensible person would have moved aside. In any other character that's a red flag; here it just reads as Lovejoy. He's halfway through the formalities when his eyes catch on the cage she's brought, and he goes quiet. The shape has registered with him before he's worked out what the shape *is*. A few scenes later, in front of an exasperated Gimbert, he sketches it onto the back wall in charcoal: a low, geometric silhouette the episode eventually reveals as a Napoleonic sea fort. The show never explains how he made the leap. That's the whole point — the divvy doesn't come with footnotes.

## Guest Star Watch

The guest cast of "The Firefly Cage" deepens the fun. Ronald Fraser, who played the doomed Drummer (bludgeoned off-screen, body washed up on the beach — a properly Lovejoy way to go), was a beloved British character actor whose CV includes *Flight of the Phoenix* (1965), *The Wild Geese* (1978), *Scandal* (1989) and a stint in *Minder*. Sarah Lawson, as Mrs Hepplestone, had been a Hammer Horror leading lady — she was menaced by Charles Gray's satanic cult in *The Devil Rides Out* (1968). She retired from acting in 1989 and lived to 95.

Kim Thomson plays the visitor with the cage — and a small confession is in order. In every memory of this episode I had before rewatching it, the woman knocking on Lovejoy's door was Lysette Anthony. It was not. Thomson is her own entirely separate proposition: a long run in *Brush Strokes*, and what feels like half of *Emmerdale* on the CV. Lysette Anthony, for the record, does turn up in *Lovejoy* later, and the apology I owe her is being deferred to that episode.

So even in episode one, behind every tweed jacket and dodgy provenance was someone who'd already been in *Star Wars*, *Roots*, *The Devils*, or had been chucked out of RADA for liking the Beatles too much. A properly mucky, brilliant, faintly disreputable cast — exactly as it should be.

## Line of the Episode

> "It's worth what someone will pay for it. That's the whole game, that is."

A throwaway line, delivered to a man who has just been told the figure and gone slightly grey. It's the show's actual thesis statement disguised as a shrug, which is a thing *Lovejoy* will keep doing for six series.

## Divvy Fact

The lacquerware MacGuffin is, in real life, a *kōbako* — an incense box. The Japanese tradition of *kōdō*, "the way of incense", treats the boxes as serious objects of artistry, sometimes more valuable than the incense inside. The show absolutely does not explain any of this. It is, however, why the prop department has been so careful with the lid.
